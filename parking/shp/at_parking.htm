Description:
Abstract:
This data set depicts parking areas along the Appalachian National Scenic Trail. It is not comprehensive, but represents all parking areas that we currently have on file.
Locational information used to create this data set were obtained from Global Positioning Systems (GPS) survey data collected between 1999-2010 using mostly Trimble GPS equipment.
Purpose:
This data set was developed to provide a depiction of parking areas along the Appalachian National Scenic Trail in an usable and easily transferable format between parties so that it can correctly be represented on digital and printed maps; to assist staff members, agency partners, and trail-maintaining clubs with trail and land management duties; and to help local planning agencies, telecommunications companies, and other groups with planning activities, such as siting new developments that could have a substantial impact on the scenic quality of the Appalachian Trail.
Supplemental_Information:
The Appalachian National Scenic Trail is a footpath over 2,170 miles in length that traverses the Appalachian Mountains from Maine to Georgia. It passes through 14 states and approximately 241 jurisdictions, and links some 75 national and state parks and forests. Virtually every mile is within easy access of a major population center and some portion of the trail is within a day's drive of 2/3rds of the U.S. population.
The idea for an Appalachian Trail was conceived by forester Benton MacKaye in 1921. In 1925, he formed the Appalachian Trail Conference (now called the Appalachian Trail Conservancy), a private not-for-profit organization whose mission is to protect, preserve, manage, and promote the Appalachian Trail. By 1937, an Appalachian Trail footpath was considered complete and open for all to enjoy. In 1968, Congress passed the National Scenic Trails Act that created a system of national scenic trails, starting with the Appalachian Trail and Pacific Crest Trail.
Though part of the national park system, the Appalachian Trail has largely remained managed by the Appalachian Trail Conservancy (formally called the Appalachian Trail Conference). This responsibility was formally delegated by the Department of the Interior in 1984. Today, the trail and its associated lands are managed by the Appalachian Trail Conservancy, its 31 affiliated trail clubs, and the National Park Service Appalachian Trail Park Office, in conjunction with several agency partners including the USDA Forest Service and numerous state park and state forest agencies.

Completeness_Report:
The Appalachian National Scenic Trail parking data was created from high accuracy Global Positioning System (GPS) data collected by Dr. Vernon Vernier (1999-2003), Karl Hartzell and John Fletcher (2005), and A.T. staff members (2001-2010), using Trimble GPS equipment, as well as some supplemental data from trail partners.
